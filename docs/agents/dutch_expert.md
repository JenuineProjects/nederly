## 3. Grammar Minimums for A0

### Articles: de / het
- Dutch has two definite articles: **de** (common gender) and **het** (neuter)
- No reliable rule ‚ÄĒ must be memorised word by word
- Rough guide: approximately 75% of nouns take *de*; *het* words must be flagged individually
- Critical *het* words at A0: het huis, het bed, het raam, het kind, het water, het gebak, het broodje, het caf√©, het station, het centrum, het ziekenhuis, het vliegveld
- Indefinite article: **een** (pronounced as schwa "uh") ‚ÄĒ same for all genders
- **Dyslexia note:** Colour-code de/het on every flashcard. Never leave the article off.

### Word Order
- Main clause: **Subject ‚ÄĒ Verb ‚ÄĒ Rest** (same as English for simple sentences)
  - *Ik drink koffie.* ‚ÄĒ I drink coffee.
- Verb-second rule: when a time word or adverb opens the sentence, the verb stays second and the subject moves:
  - *Morgen ga ik naar de winkel.* ‚ÄĒ Tomorrow go I to the shop.
- Questions: verb moves to first position:
  - *Drink jij koffie?* ‚ÄĒ Do you drink coffee?
- At A0, teach only main-clause SVO. Subordinate clause verb-final is A1+.

### Zijn (to be) ‚ÄĒ Present Tense
| Person | Form |
|--------|------|
| ik | ben |
| jij / je | bent |
| hij/zij/het | is |
| wij/jullie/zij | zijn |

### Hebben (to have) ‚ÄĒ Present Tense
| Person | Form |
|--------|------|
| ik | heb |
| jij / je | hebt |
| hij/zij/het | heeft |
| wij/jullie/zij | hebben |

### Negation
- **niet** negates verbs and adjectives: *Ik drink niet.* / *Het is niet lekker.*
- **geen** negates nouns (replaces the article): *Ik heb geen koffie.* (not: *Ik heb niet een koffie.*)
- Position: *niet* goes to the end of the clause for verbs; *geen* replaces the article directly before the noun.

## 5. Register Notes ‚ÄĒ Formal (u) vs Informal (je/jij)

### When to use formal *u*
- With strangers, shop staff, waiters (first contact) ‚ÄĒ especially anyone over approximately 40
- In professional or official settings
- When the other person uses *u* with you first
- Written correspondence to unknown recipients
- Restaurant survival phrases addressed to a waiter: *Wat raadt u aan? / Heeft u ook appeltaart?*

### When to use informal *je/jij*
- With peers, friends, classmates
- With children
- In casual settings once rapport is established
- Most younger Dutch people (under approximately 35) default to *je* in almost all contexts
- The Lesson 2 dialogue models both registers: the waiter uses *je* in casual mode (*Ben je al klaar?*) and *u* in formal mode ‚ÄĒ both occur naturally in real Dutch caf√©s

### Practical A0 rule
- Default to **je** with people your age or younger
- Default to **u** with anyone serving you formally (waiter, shopkeeper, doctor, receptionist) until they signal otherwise
- Switching from *u* to *je* mid-conversation is normal ‚ÄĒ follow the other person's lead
- **Lesson 2 note:** *Mag ik de rekening, alstublieft?* uses the formal *alstublieft* ‚ÄĒ correct for addressing a waiter you do not know. *Alsjeblieft* is the informal equivalent.

## 7. Content Recommendations for Spaced Post-Lesson Practice

Based on Researcher ¬ß2 (engagement strategies) and ¬ß4 (dyslexia considerations).

### Session structure
- **5‚Äď15 minutes per session, daily** ‚ÄĒ distributed practice outperforms weekly marathons for vocabulary retention (Researcher ¬ß2, Cambridge Core study on distributed practice)
- **One category per session** ‚ÄĒ cognitive load stays manageable; dyslexic learners benefit from short, single-focus modules (Researcher ¬ß4)
- **Interleave old and new** ‚ÄĒ after Lesson 2, every session should include some Les 1 words. Suggested ratio: 60% new lesson / 40% previous lessons

### What to prioritise on flashcards
- Always show: Dutch word, article (de/het), English gloss, IPA, respelling, ‚ėÖ flag where applicable
- For ‚ėÖ words: audio is essential ‚ÄĒ text description alone is insufficient (Researcher ¬ß7, Dutch Sound Reference Guide internal 2026-05-20)
- Phonetic hint position: immediately below the Dutch word, not below the translation (Researcher ¬ß7 evidence-based layout pattern)

### Retrieval practice over re-reading
- Seeing the Dutch word and actively producing the English (or vice versa) is far more effective than passively re-reading a list (Researcher ¬ß2, Frontiers in Education 2025 study)
- Mix directions: Dutch ‚Üí English and English ‚Üí Dutch in the same session
- 3‚Äď4 relearning sessions per word within 14 days produces substantially better recall than fewer sessions (Researcher ¬ß2)

### Dyslexia-specific adjustments
- Colour-code de/het on every card ‚ÄĒ reduces working memory load (Researcher ¬ß4)
- Keep phonetic hints in the same position on every card ‚ÄĒ learners should not have to search for it (Researcher ¬ß7)
- Avoid IPA-only display ‚ÄĒ plain English respelling outperforms IPA symbols in accessibility for beginners and dyslexic learners (Researcher ¬ß7)
- Immediate, specific feedback after each card (correct/incorrect + brief reason) aids adult dyslexic learners more than delayed feedback (Researcher ¬ß4)
- No time pressure ‚ÄĒ adequate time to process each card is a significant factor for dyslexic learners (Researcher ¬ß4)

### Category sequencing within a session
1. Warm-up with easiest category (Greetings, Numbers 1‚Äď10)
2. Main block: lesson-specific vocabulary (Les 1 or Les 2 categories)
3. End with a mixed-category round that includes ‚ėÖ words ‚ÄĒ these need the most repetition
4. Survival phrases last ‚ÄĒ multi-word items have highest cognitive load

### Lesson 2 practice priorities
- *graag*, *nog*, *contant* are high-frequency function words that appear across many real situations ‚ÄĒ target these for rapid automaticity
- The Lesson 2 caf√© dialogue provides a natural sentence context for all 30 vocabulary words ‚ÄĒ use full-phrase cards (not just single-word) for the Zinnen sub-category
- Formal vs informal contrast (*alsjeblieft* vs *alstublieft*, *dank je wel* vs *dank u wel*) is worth a dedicated mini-round once core vocabulary is secure
- *nog* and *lekker* are polysemous ‚ÄĒ flag them as "one word, many meanings" when first introduced (Researcher ¬ß1 trap 12)
